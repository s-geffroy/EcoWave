\begin{abstract}
We test whether the four canonical economic cycles---Kitchin (3--5 years),
Juglar (7--11 years), Kuznets (15--25 years) and Kondratieff (40--60
years)---are statistically distinguishable from coloured noise across six
heterogeneous macroeconomic panels spanning 1700--2024 (Bank of England
Millennium, J\`ord\`a-Schularick-Taylor R6, Bank for International
Settlements credit aggregates, World Bank annual indicators, FRED/Eurostat
quarterly, and a sectoral panel). A pre-registered three-gate falsifiable
protocol is applied to every cell of the
\(\{\text{cycle}\}\times\{\text{panel}\}\times\{\text{variable}\}\) grid:
(i) Gate~1 requires the band-pass power in the canonical band to exceed
both an \(\AR{1}\)-bootstrap null and a phase-randomised null at
\(\alpha=0.05\) with \(B=1000\) surrogates; (ii) Gate~2 requires consensus
between at least three of four heterogeneous estimators
(Christiano-Fitzgerald band-pass with Hilbert phase, Bry-Boschan turning
points, Hamilton-style Markov-switching \(\AR{1}\) and PELT changepoints);
(iii) Gate~3 requires universality across at least four of five income
aggregates. A per-variable safeguard reproduces \citet{wen2005} and
removes composite-aggregation artefacts. None of the four cycles survives
the cascade on more than a small minority of panels, and Kondratieff is
the most fragile: when the safeguard is enforced, zero individual
variables survive on the long-history panels. The negative result is not
a methodological failure---it reproduces a long-standing empirical
consensus \citep{garvy1943,solomou1987,romer1999,stock_watson2003,wen2005}.
The paper ends by pointing to an alternative empirical signature
(long memory, multifractality, non-linearity, permutation entropy and
reflexive drift), developed in a companion methodological paper.
\end{abstract}
