\begin{abstract}
We apply a single uniform protocol to the four canonical economic
cycles (Kitchin 3--5 years, Juglar 7--11 years, Kuznets 15--25
years, Kondratieff 40--60 years) across five independent
macroeconomic panels spanning 1700--2024
(Bank of England Millennium, J\`ord\`a-Schularick-Taylor R6, World
Bank annual aggregates, FRED/Eurostat/OECD/BIS quarterly, sectoral
historical). On a (panel, group, variable, cycle) grid of more
than 1\,400 testable cells we find that canonical cycles are
\emph{empirically alive} on exactly the variables the substantive
literature predicts: the Juglar band passes Gate~1 on investment
and unemployment across the J\`ord\`a-Schularick-Taylor advanced
economies (2.2-fold excess over null on 605 cells); the Kuznets
band passes on house prices (46\,\% of testable JST countries),
on population and on credit aggregates (1.9-fold excess); the
Kitchin band passes on emerging-market credit aggregates on the
quarterly BIS panel (5.3-fold excess on 93 cells); and the
Kondratieff band passes on UK public debt / GDP over 1700--2016
(Reinhart-Rogoff debt-cycle reading). These concentrations
empirically vindicate \citet{kitchin1923}, \citet{juglar1862},
\citet{kuznets1930}, \citet{schumpeter1939} and \citet{borio2014}
on the channels each named. What our protocol \emph{rejects} is
the strong universalist reading: no single canonical period
describes \emph{all} macroeconomic variables across heterogeneous
panels, and the variable-specific concentrations do not survive a
strict Benjamini-Hochberg false-discovery-rate adjustment on the
joint grid at our surrogate counts. The negative result is
properly scoped to the universalist sinusoidal-on-everything
reading; the positive result vindicates the substantive theory of
each cycle on the variable each was originally claimed to govern.
\end{abstract}
